Adoption Blog: Improv Mom

A Breakthrough Visit
Posted by Barbara Herel to Improv Mom 3 days, 4 hours ago | 1 Comment | Post a reply
Filed Under: Adoption Conversations, Birthparent Connections, Domestic Adoption, Open Adoption


This, my friends, this was the trip I’ve been waiting for. Here is one of the most important things that happened on our fourth visit with Beth’s birth family:

Kim and I started talking about Beth’s adoption.

We were on the playground and I clumsily blurted out, “Heeey…do you want to…feel up to talking about the adoption at all?” Boy, did she ever.

Kim launched into it, asking if I had been afraid she would change her mind about the adoption before it was final. I told her yes, it was something I had thought about, knew it was a possibility, knew she had every right to, and that all I could do was keep telling myself, “This could work.”

I asked if I should stop leaving her phone messages since she doesn’t call back. Kim explained that, as excited as she is to talk/text in the weeks leading up to seeing us, talking/texting in-between visits just feels “different.” I can see how a “Just calling to say Hi” phone call with no visit on the horizon could make Kim feel her loss, her pain, much more sharply…leaving her with a deep and lingering ache that could only be soothed by being with Beth. Kim did say, “The messages do make my day though.” Even the ones where Beth chimes in with a, “Love you, Poopy Head,” so, yes, I will continue to leave them for the pure joy of it and without any expectations.

Kim told me she loved how we were raising Beth (Awww, I loved hearing that!). She also loved how outgoing Beth is and how she fits right into the mix during our visits. (And it is a big mix—Beth and her three siblings, plus Kim’s boyfriend has three kids, and Kim’s sister has two—totaling nine kids.) As far as Beth’s outgoing personality, I said she thrives in a crowd; she’s a born extrovert extraordinaire. I did take credit for the child being polite and encouraging her inquisitiveness and daring nature, though.

I asked Kim how she was feeling about the adoption today. She shared with me once again that, as soon as she read our profile book, she “knew” we were the people to raise her baby. Kim did expand on the story a bit—once she knew her choice, she handed the pile of books to her sister to see who she would choose, and they both picked us. Kim reiterated that she doesn’t feel regret. She loves our visits and couldn’t imagine having an adoption where she couldn’t see Beth; that only getting pictures and updates could never, ever, be enough.

That’s about all the burning adoption questions we got around to before the kids started infiltrating our space, as kids do whenever the talk gets juicy. Before being completely overrun, I quickly mentioned to Kim that Beth is definitely the kind of kid who is going to want to know all the Whys one day, probably sooner than later, and I’d like to help Kim set the stage for that.

Now sure, I know the broad-stroke Whys and I’ve been telling Beth things like, “Kim and Charlie weren’t able to care for any baby at that time.” Yet certainly, there must be other reasons that led Kim and Charlie to make an adoption plan. I don’t want to make assumptions. I do want Beth to know the truthful tangible details beyond my cookie-cutter adoption responses.

I feel encouraged and confident that Kim and I will be able to strike up an adoption conversation when we next meet. But can I really ask Kim to tell all to me? Is it necessary, or, even right of me, to ask this of her so I can process it and introduce it to Beth first?
