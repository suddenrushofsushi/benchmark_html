122. Agriculture Hymn

As usual, I came up with Too Many Stanzas. Oh, well! It was just such a, ahem, fertile field of inspiration! On the other hand, I'm sure somebody will object that I missed an important branch of agriculture - but how long does this hymn really need to be?

O God, whose word like living seed
First broke creation's virgin soil,
It is a little thing indeed
That You should bless the farmer's toil.

You who move mountains by Your hand
And crush the stony heart to dust,
Give unto those who break the land
Both fruitful toil and restful trust!

Give unto those who till and sow
The strength of hope and patience sure!
Come flood or famine, hail or snow,
You yet will bless all who endure.

As You, Lord, sometimes blend with pain
The lessons leading us to light,
Those who the growth must prune and train
Make wise to judge its limits right!

When You, Lord, cleanse the hearts of men,
Their darkest stains show bright and clean;
How will You then forgo to send
The rain that keeps the croplands green?

Encourage us when weed takes root,
When field is rank with rot and pest;
For though we yielded bitter fruit,
You, Lord, forgave and promised rest.

You sent a Bread made without hands,
Life-giving, living, from above;
So let the bread raised from our lands
Reflect Your sacrificial Love!

You sent, O Lord, a living Vine
In whom as branches we are spliced;
So in our grafting and our wine
Remind us that we are in Christ!

From things that captivate and damn,
O Lord, You saved Your straying sheep;
Let us recall this with each lamb
We lead to pasture, watch and keep!

We find in Your pure word, O Lord,
A milk to nourish heart and soul;
So may there also here be poured
The milk that keeps our bodies whole!

Our flax and wool, our fowl and egg,
Choice meat, plump grain and sweetest comb
Come all from You, and so we beg,
Uphold us till our harvest-home!

Tears of the Giraffe

Tears of the Giraffe
by Alexander McCall Smith
Recommended Ages: 14+

In the second casebook of The No. 1 Ladies' Detective Agency, Mma Ramotswe once again wrestles with the case of a missing son whose trail has gone cold. This time it's a 20-year-old American boy whose parents left him behind when they returned home from diplomatic duty in Botswana because he had such a heart for Africa that he didn't want to leave.

For a couple years they heard regularly from him as he worked on an experimental farm. Then, suddenly, he vanished and for ten years, not a trace of him could be found. Neither the police nor an expert tracker nor the American embassy could help. Only now does the boy's mother learn try what a native Botswana woman can do with a high school education, a strong intuition and a few months' experience as a self-taught investigator.

While Mma Ramotswe sniffs the cold trail of the no doubt long dead youth, there are plenty of other things to occupy her mind. She is engaged to marry Mr. J. L. B. Matekoni, owner of an auto repair shop. Her family is about to grow in an unexpected way, thanks to her fiance's soft heart toward orphans. Her secretary, newly promoted to assistant private detective, has solved her first case but in so doing, found herself in an ethical dilemma. And a jealous housemaid is hatching an evil plot against Mma Ramotswe.

These storylines unspool with lyrical grace, gentle humor and a touch of spooky suspense. And it's not hard to sense where the heart of the story is located, when the moment Mma Ramotswe realizes she's about to become the mother of two orphaned children proves more suspenseful than her interview with the sociopath responsible for the American boy's death.

If like me you enjoy the charming world this series of mysteries opens up, you'll be glad to know there are fourteen more books to follow this, starting with Morality for Beautiful Girls. Other series penned by the same author include the "Harriet Bean," "Max and Maddy" and "Akimbo" children's books, each set ranging from three to eight books; a quartet of "Professor Dr Moritz-Maria von Igelfeld" books starting with Portuguese Irregular Verbs, ten "Isabel Dalhousie" books starting with The Sunday Philosophy Club; another 10-book series named after its initial book 44 Scotland Street; the Corduroy Mansions trilogy; a quartet of Precious Ramotswe children's books; and more than 20 other novels, including some for kids, with such titles as The Ice Cream Bicycle and La's Orchestra Saves the World.

121. Journey Hymn

Here is a hymn that prays, on one level, for those who travel, and perhaps on another level for those who stray from the faith. Because this possible double usefulness is hard enough to build into a hymn text, I am sparing myself the trouble of inventing a new tune for it by repurposing one I previously wrote for a hymn by my friend Alan. It's called ASPICE PROTECTOR.

Lord, who led Jacob's seed,
From Egypt's bondage freed,
By signs of cloud and burning:
When now Your children roam
Guide them in safety home,
In peace and joy returning.

As wise men from afar
You beckoned by a star
To view Your Son and Mary's,
So also walk beside
Those who go forth, and guide
Them where Your presence tarries.

Such signs they may not see
As You lent formerly
To journeys great and solemn;
Be, Lord, Yourself their light
Showing them wrong from right,
Your Word their fiery column.

No star now marks the place
Where they may find Your grace,
Still less the seeking stranger;
Yet Your blest Sacrament
Shows sinners who repent
A rest from toil and danger.

Now over floods of grief
No dove-flown olive leaf
Bespeaks an end to frowning;
Yet when we here baptize,
You hold before our eyes
The death of death, sin's drowning.

No cross need mark the place
Where You redeemed our race,
Once for all people dying;
Where we proclaim Your death
You dwell, bestowing faith
And sinners justifying.

So on this latter day
Draw us along Your way,
To life and glory pressing;
Help us to run our race,
Your all-availing grace
With lips and lives confessing.

The No. 1 Ladies' Detective Agency

The No. 1 Ladies' Detective Agency
by Alexander McCall Smith
Recommended Ages: 14+

This is the first book in a series by the same name, featuring Botswana's first female private detective, Mma Precious Ramotswe. At this writing there are approximately 16 books in this series, including such interesting titles as Morality for Beautiful Girls, Tea Time for the Traditionally Built and The Minor Adjustment Beauty Salon.

Botswana is a dry, sparsely populated country in southern Africa surrounded by Namibia, Zimbabwe and South Africa. Formerly a British protectorate, it became independent in 1966 when Mma Ramotswe was eight years old. In the decades between then and the mid-1990s when this story is set, it has evolved into one of the most successful countries in Africa, with a vibrant economy and lots of modern improvements. Mma Ramotswe (the Mma means approximately "Mrs.") is proud of her country, and she is proud to be its first female private detective.

Though this inaugural book of her adventures collects several of her early cases together, including some work for rich and powerful clients that help to establish her in business, her first big case involves the disappearance of a village schoolmaster's son.

The boy has been missing two years before Mma Ramotswe gets the case, and she has little doubt the boy is dead and she doesn't expect to be able to solve it. But it niggles at her. She senses that traditional witchcraft may be behind it, something that is both terrifyingly dangerous to go against and a shameful stain on all the modern progress her country has made. And then she gets a lead that may connect the death of this child to one of the most powerful men in the country - the type of man who can make life very difficult for those who cross him.

Along the way she solves the case of a missing husband, finds proof of a cheating ditto, clears up a hospital administrator's suspicions about one of his doctors, solves a case of insurance fraud, and uses her woman's intuition and native resourcefulness to supplement her lessons from a manual of private detection. She gets mixed up with an anxious father and his over-protected daughter. She tracks down a missing dog. She more or less perfects her technique of tailing a suspect, learns to tell a well-turned lie to get information or avoid trouble, and develops a reputation as a woman who can get things done.

In between charming vignettes from the early days of a detective agency are passages describing African life in such sensuous and heartfelt terms that you feel the warmth of the author's love for the land. You can almost smell the smoke of wood fires in the remote cattle stations, taste the dry air of the Kalahari, see the vivid color of the sunsets, and feel a trickle of sweat in the October heat. Though the book's author is now a Scotsman, this book is a potent reminder that he grew up in Zimbabwe, right next door to the Botswana depicted here, and like a character in this book's sequel Tears of the Giraffe, he seems to have a heart for Africa.

As for what this book is, it moves along in bite-sized episodes, like a series of short fictions, and sometimes takes a break from mystery to deliver personal memoirs of Mma Ramotswe, her late father, and their country that have the ring almost of lyrical nonfiction - that fact-and-fiction-blending type of popular journalism that some authors call "faction." A romance with Mr. J. L. B. Matekoni, a master auto mechanic, and the underlying mystery of the missing boy help tie it all together as a novel. And an audiobook narrated by Lisette Lecat, herself an African expatriate with a quiverful of ethnic dialects, puts the last perfection on an altogether pleasurable reading experience. I will be visiting Mma Ramotswe's agency again.

Notes of Interest 11

Here are the June 2015 numbers of my weekly column in The Morgan County Press of Stover, Mo.

Wednesday, June 3: Support the Stover Fair

One thing I have learned from a lifelong study of grammar is that the future perfect is a hard verb tense to sell. Maybe it’s because a deep instinct tells us the future isn’t written, and once it becomes the past, it can’t be changed.

Take this sentence for example: By the time you read this, the Stover Chamber of Commerce will have decided either to go ahead with the Stover Fair Queen, Youth Queen, and Junior Miss contests or to scrub them and organize a talent show instead. It depends on you.

Or rather, it will have depended on you. Phew! Realistically as well as grammatically, that’s pretty awkward.

It’s too late for this story to make any difference in the outcome of the chamber’s dilemma. But it isn’t too late to do something about the 2015 Stover Fair.

It’s going to happen Thursday through Saturday, June 18-20 at Legion Memorial Park in Stover. A lot of information about it is already available online at stoverfair.org. A nice new carnival is coming to town, and it’s not going to cost Stover anything whether you go or not.

But if you don’t go, the carnival won’t come back. Indeed, if you don’t go, it’s possible the Stover Fair may not come back.

As someone at the Thursday, May 28 chamber meeting put it, “Step up or it’s gone.”

The speaker was talking about taking part in fair events, such as contests and entertainment. But she could just as correctly put it, “Show up or it’s gone.”

I won’t patronize you with all-knowing theories about why people don’t participate in community activities like they used to. I won’t blame you for liking what you like, or not liking what you don’t like. I won’t tax you for planning something else for your family that week, such as a camping trip or a beach vacation or a class reunion out of town.

But if you happen to be at home during Stover Fair Days, I hope you’ll show support for a local tradition that builds community and could, if only you take part in it, be a lot of fun.

While I’m making requests, let me also lift up two needs I have noted as various groups discussed their fair plans.

First: If you can help in any way, don’t wait to be asked. Nobody has time to call everybody in the 65078 zip code and beg them to do each specific thing that needs done. Instead, why not step forward and ask what you can do?

Second: If you’ve got kids or grandkids lying around the house this summer, please do all in your power to encourage them to go to the Stover Fair.

For example, unplug the TV. Take the batteries out of their electronic devices. Give them a wad of money. Lock them out of the house and tell them not to come back without a stamp on their hand or paint on their face.

Bribe them, blackmail them, threaten them with awful punishments like having to eat corn on the cob without butter all summer, or having to wear a Chicago Cubs cap. Do whatever is in the limits of decency to move them from the couch to the carnival.

When there’s a Stover Fair for their kids to enjoy, they will bless you for it.

Wednesday, June 10: Feedback feedback

Now and then, or maybe several times a week, we miss our opporunity to get a story into the newspaper. Here is a taste of some of the stories we’ve missed lately.

An unidentified lady phone our office last week to point out we did not have any stories in the Wednesday, June 3 issue mentioning D-Day, which was Saturday, June 6.

It is indeed an important day to remember. June 6, 1944 is his widely considered the turning point of World War II, when the Allied forces invaded Axis-occupied France and began pushing Hitler’s forces back.

I apologize for overlooking this historic observance. I would add not as an excuse, but as an explanation: the big blotter on my desk, with the calendar recognizing such important dates as Armed Forces Day (May 16), First of Ramadan (June 18) and Father’s Day/Summer begins (June 21), makes no mention of D-Day. With no local organizations holding events to mark the day, it was easy to overlook.

Once again it shows I can use everybody’s help remembering things that may not automatically be on my calendar.

If an important event is coming up, please feel free to give us a call at the newspaper office - 573-378-5441 - and make sure we’re aware, so we can give it the coverage it deserves.

I know Flag Day is going to be Sunday, June 14. I haven’t heard about any local events being planned to celebrate it. Maybe in a quiet, unannounced way people will simply make sure their nation’s colors are clean, trim looking and flying that day.

Several people have written, phoned and spoken to me in person in recent weeks to give encouraging feedback on how the newspaper looks. I thank them, and I also thank the staff at the newspaper office in Versailles and the press room in Eldon for making the Press look good.

I especially want to thank layout expert and graphic design wonder worker Troy Sinclair, who will be leaving the Vernon Publishing family soon to pursue a different career. Best of luck!

Elvera Siegel of Florence wrote me last week to share some stories. One of them was about Joyce McCormick of Versailles, whose daughters Eunice Officer and Lois Weber live in Stover.

“Joyce and her husband had a coon hunting business,” Siegel wrote. “Men would stay in their home, rent their mules and her husband would guide them on coon hunting jaunts. Sometimes Joyce would join them on her own mule. She was also handy with a gun.

“Joyce cooked for the men and evidently she was a good cook. One day she found a $100 tip under a plate when she was cleaning the table. You would never think of this eighty-some-year-old little lady as someone who could ride a mule and shoot coons with the best of them.”

Thanks for the stories, Mrs. Siegel!

Wednesday, June 17: More feedback feedback

Marty Weber asked me last week to consider adding a Letters to the Editor feature to this newspaper.

It’s an interesting idea to think about, though at the moment we’re not hurting for material to fill the space. For the time being, though, I find people’s letters helpful as inspiration for my personal column.

Last week I mentioned a story Elvera Siegel told in a letter she wrote me. Space did not permit me to include another story that I thought was equally interesting.

“My grandson Aric,” Siegel wrote, “had an unusual tale to tell his second grade class last February.

“He spotted an eagle flying only about four feet above the pasture where he and his dad were feeding cattle. The eagle had something dangling on its foot.”

Aric’s father Chris Siegel called a conservation agent, who then called someone Aric described to his grandmother as “the eagle man.”

“They came out to the farm and finally caught the poor bird,” Elvera went on. “Aric got to help hold the eagle down while the ‘eagle man’ examined him. The eagle had a coyote trap clamped to his foot.”

They found it interesting that even though the family never had a coyote trap and the eagle didn’t belong to them, Chris Siegel had to sign a paper allowing the conservationist to remove the bird from his property.

“Chris looked for feathers so Aric could take one to school,” Elvera wrote. “He found none, but was told he couldn’t keep it had he found one. Only Native Americans are allowed to have feathers. The conservation department has a long list of people to call when they do have feathers.”

See? Where would this column be this week without that letter? Thanks, Mrs. Siegel!

While I’m sharing feedback from our readers, Lurleen Holem phoned in last week when she received a late renewal notice from our office. Evidently her first bill to renew her subscription went astray in the mail.

She asked me to share with everyone this reasonable request: “If people get someone else’s mail, put it back in the mail. Don’t just throw it away, even if it looks like junk.”

In an off-the-agenda chat with members of the Stover Fair Board Tuesday, June 9, I learned there is a consensus that the worst of the road construction on Highway 135 isn’t the one-lane traffic but a huge pothole just a few miles south of Stover. I have to agree. For more than a week it has stood full of loose paving material. My car bottoms out every time I drive over it, spraying rubble in all directions. The hole is as wide as the southbound lane and only a little shorter than my Volkswagen. I hope it gets attention from the road workers soon.

Chuck Morris brought me an idea for a story that hasn’t gone anywhere. Since I first came to Stover, I’ve often wondered what that curved mirror gizmo is that sits on a boat trailer next to Great Southern Bank. He said it was designed to convert solar energy into electricity.

If anyone else can point me to a source who will discuss where that artifact came from and what its future may hold, please call me at 573-378-5441.

Last week I met Aaron Fowler outside the Southern Missouri Wood Products building on First Street in Stover. I thought a candid photo of his workers sweating shirtlessly in the sun might boost circulation a bit, but he hinted a story about him could be a career killer.

It’s funny how sneaky traps like that can lie hidden in nice small towns. Stover just keeps getting more interesting!

Wednesday, June 24: Stover Fair

The 2015 Stover Fair got off to a moist start.

A week of almost daily rainstorms culminated in flooding that washed out roads and a squall every six hours or so, remnants of Tropical Storm Bob.

I spent my lunch break Thursday, June 18 chasing reports of washed out low-water bridges. I saw a couple that had streams flowing just under them, and some hills where runoff was carving creases in previously well-graded gravel.

One bridge on Pyrmont Road showed evidence of a large tree falling and taking out the approach to a bridge, but the road had already been repaired when I got there.

As usually happens when I drive the gravel roads west and north of Stover, the beauty of the scenery took my breath away - even under gloomy storm clouds and on roads whose swooping curves turned my knuckles white on the steering wheel.

I didn’t get the pictures I wanted, but maybe word-pictures will make up for them.

There’s an abandoned old house on Pyrmont Road that looked like the kind of place professional photographers seek out and shoot for collectible calendars and coffee-table books. A two-story gray thing with loose shingles and gaps between its boards, it looks like the kind of place that would have a story to tell, if walls could talk.

Through gaps in the trees, there are many wonderful glimpses of rolling fields. Wildflowers crowd along fences along the roadside. Around every curve there seems to be another pretty barn, a gateway decorated with wrought-iron designs, or even a donkey staring insolently back at passersby.

Every time I stopped my car, or even started slowing down, to take a picture of road workers repairing washed-out ends of driveways along the highway, the skies opened and rain drove me back into the vehicle.

It even threatened to rain on me while I was helping judge the little king, queen, prince and princess contests Thursday, July 18 at the Stover Fair. I joined Isom Folsom and Darcy Jones, co-owner of the Jones & Co. Carnival, at the judges’ table, but I had to keep putting my camera back in its bag between shots of cute children to keep the drizzle off it. I thought it was really nice of Jones to offer free carnival passes to all the contestants.

The notepaper I was writing on got soaked and started falling apart. I even lost a page of notes. If you find it, don’t worry about it. I don’t need it any more.

The weather wasn’t very cooperative. Some of the Stover Fair’s plans had to be changed on short notice. But there were early signs it would turn out to be a well attended fair anyway.

Good sized crowds turned out for the junior royalty contests and the talent contest Thursday evening. The activity in the carnival showed the mud wasn’t keeping children away from the rides. And though traffic had yet to pick up in the quilt show, the exhibit brought a touch of class to a rustic, hometown gathering.

If you didn’t see Noah Whittle painting fantasy landscapes at the “I Spray Art,” booth, you missed something neat. The Stover high school student used spraying, brushing and smearing moves to paint magical scenes in a whirl of improvisation.

The American Legion food stand served hamburgers that transported me back to the county fair of my childhood - thick, perfectly round disks of drippy, hand-pressed goodness.

Angie Robinson gave a nice rendition of the national anthem during the color guard ceremony while local sprout Cory Garr, who starts law school in the fall, served as emcee. Keep an eye on that guy. He’ll be your Congressman someday.

The parade, though short and diverted away from the park, had some floats that made good use of the theme “Where the Rainbow Ends.” The question of the hour was, “When will the rainbow begin?” But that was just the beginning of the fun.

As Chimney Sweepers Come to Dust

As Chimney Sweepers Come to Dust
by Alan Bradley
Recommended Ages: 12+

When her widowed father and spinster aunt send her away to a boarding school in Canada, twelve-year-old English heiress, chemist and sleuth Flavia de Luce feels as if she is being exiled. But on her first night at Miss Bodycote's Female Academy in Toronto, a desiccated corpse wrapped in a Union Jack, with the wrong head attached, drops out of the chimney into her dorm room and makes her feel right at home again.

Flavia's dogged pursuit of whodunit strains even the level of tolerance for rule-breaking afforded a girl whose family fortune is really what keeps the school doors open. It threatens to upset the delicate cover of a school that is partly, secretly, a training facility for secret agents, of whom Flavia is expected to be one. It leads her to pry into the affairs of the faculty, the regents, even the laundresses who work in the shed behind the school's main building.

Tied somehow into the murder of the body in the chimney are the disappearances of three schoolgirls two years earlier, and a mysterious sighting of one of them just recently; the fishy (sorry) drowning of the chairman's first wife, whose body was never found; suspicious circumstances surrounding another student's drugging and hospitalization; and the fact that Flavia's favorite teacher, acquitted of murder in the poisoning death of her husband, has been arrested even though she seems the least likely suspect in this case.

Flavia and murder go together like bangers and mash, but without Buckshaw and its eccentric inhabitants, something is definitely missing. Flavia herself misses it desperately. And though it seems a tragedy to deprive this brilliant, precocious girl of a good education, washing out of school seems to be the best thing for her. At least, it will be good for us, as things will evidently go back to normal - with a certain difference - in time for the eighth book in the series, The Curious Case of the Copper Corpse.
