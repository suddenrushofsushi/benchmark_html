PAIN-Listening to Your Body

by CavemanGreg on May 11, 2011

I was just driving home from my first physical therapy session to rehabilitate my right shoulder (it was dislocated in a cycling accident 8 weeks ago) and I started to think about pain. I knew going into rehab that it would likely be a bit uncomfortable and painful trying to restore range of motion to my immobile shoulder, but good Lord almighty I didn’t think I would start getting light headed, twitching uncontrollably, and breathing erratically. The PT showed me little mercy and was pushing and pulling my arm either which way to try and loosen up all the adhesions that have resulted from the healing process. It was an extremely painful process. The last time I felt this much pain was from the accident itself.

I consider myself to be a pretty tough guy. I excel in 24 hour mountain bike races, I can do hours of tough physical labor, and I can tolerate all kinds of weather extremes. Yet, here I was crying uncle at a half hour PT session. For whatever reason, I just started thinking about the phenomenon of pain. What is it? Why do we feel it? Is it good or bad? And then it just sort of hit me: pain is our body’s way of saying something is wrong. Surely it is some kind of evolutionary measure designed to keep us away from harm, and thus sustain our species.

Let’s face it, the human body is a frail thing. In our minds, we might think we’re pretty tough and amazing and that we are the superior species of the earth. I’ll never forget the words of world renown mountain climber Reinhold Messner, the first person to ever summit Mt. Everest without supplemental oxygen. He said something to the effect that “humans can summit 29,000 feet peaks, but with extreme difficulty. Birds can fly along easily at this altitude. We can crest steep rocky, icy slopes but need all kinds of special gear. A mountain goat can simply dash right up. He doesn’t need shoes or clothing. We can sprint at maybe 20 mph, but a cheetah can sprint 70mph” It’s interesting how wild animals really make us look like wussies, but it’s the truth. Luckily we have greater brainpower and opposable thumbs to help compensate for our lack of physical prowess.

Taking our larger brains into consideration, we know that fire will hurt us, so will exposure to extreme cold, so do poisonous insects, so do sharp things like thorns or knives, so will hard impacts-just to name a few perils. There is no doubt in our minds that these things will injure or kill us, and that’s why we instinctively try to avoid them. What about the pain that is associated with chronic cardio or endurance activities? For many of us, there must be a short circuit in our brains that actually draws us to pain! Somehow, this physical signal of inevitable danger is a desirable thing. “No pain, no gain. Pain is weakness leaving the body. Pain is temporary, glory lasts forever.” Insert your favorite pain cliche here (or in the comments section). If you have a Western view of sports and fitness, you undoubtedly think these kinds of pain are a good thing and that society rewards them. Are they though? Is the risk worth the reward? That answer is ultimately up to you, but please allow me to share my thoughts.

Scores of endurance athletes get injured each year due to overuse. Think about all the terms we’ve coined for such injuries: tendonitis, plantar fasciitis, bunions, shin splints, stress fractures, bursitis, ligament tears (ACL is common), IT band syndrome, runner’s knee, etc. etc. All of theses injuries are incurred in the name of “fitness”. Somewhere along the lines of physical education, the school of thought that “more is better” became commonplace. People have this perception that running marathons, doing long bike races, and competing  in ironmans are ultimate benchmarks of fitness. I know because I used to be of the same mindset. Things are starting to change though. Along with the Paleo/primal/caveman models of exercise, you see fitness groups popping up like Crossfit, bootcamps, and HIIT ( High Intensity Interval Training) On TV you see less machines that emphasize hours of cardio workout, but you see more devices that are designed to work you hard for 6-20 minutes at high intensity and you’re done! I predict more and more sports research is going to come out validating what hunter-gatherers have known for millenia: endurance kills, sprinting builds.

So, if you’re out doing 3 hour rides or 17 mile runs trying to train for the next big race, and you find yourself asking is this really healthy for me? or is this pain a good thing? or isn’t there a better way to do this that involves less time and less pain but better results? , then perhaps you are starting to actually listen to those pain signals from your body and realize that true fitness and injury prevention comes from following the Paleo Exercise Laws. It doesn’t come from better running shoes, prescribed workout regiments, or diagnostic tools like heart rate monitors and power meters.  Walking for a few hours each week surely doesn’t hurt, sprinting only hurts a little while you stop to catch your breath (but feels really good afterwards), and sporadically lifting heavy things doesn’t cause much pain other than grunting, unless you have horrible technique or are lifting beyond your means.

Keep it simple, keep it short (or keep it easy if you’re walking), keep it fun, and keep it pain free, because I for one am a believer that pain is a bad thing.
