School's done, we are back in the states


After 10 wonderful weeks in Peru all three of us are back in the United States.  Gabe and Michael were able to enjoy some travels through South America (Lake Titicaca and Salar de Uyuni to name a few) before they made it back to the states.  I, lamentably, was unable to join them as I had to fly back to get ready for my next commitment, training for FrancisCorps, which will send me to Costa Rica for a year.  Gabe and Michael set off for their adventure on Thursday, August 11 and I didn’t fly out until Monday morning.  I was able to see the boys off at the airport and then moved in with the neighbors for my last three nights.  I was lonely in our apartment.  It just wasn’t the same being in Peru without Gabe and Michael!

The community planned a nice despedida for us complete with food, champagne, and parting gifts.  All three of us received new “chompas,” or sweaters, that the community picked out especially for us.  Gabe’s was a little too small, and he made everyone laugh by trying to slip it over his head without unzipping it.  Everyone sat around to eat the food the mothers had made and, as usual, the gringos were given HUGE plates of food that none of us could finish.  Really, during 10 weeks in Peru I have never been force fed so much in my life.  I never really understood why they always thought the three of us always needed to eat way more than anyone else.   Profesora Sabina made a nice toast and we responded with our heartfelt thanks for the community.  With so many children and parents there it really was a great goodbye party.

Leaving the Catorce de Febrero community was very sad for all of us.  The children and families were sad to see us leave and were filling us up with food and goodbyes until the very last minute.  On Gabe and Michael’s last day we were about to leave when two mothers stopped us and insisted they cook us lunch.  They said they would cook very fast and that it would be better for the boys to leave with a good meal in their bellies.  We ended up staying about an hour later than we had planned waiting for our arroz con pollo to finish.  (This was after we had already been invited for a late breakfast of arroz con leche with mazamorra morada).  Thankfully, we anticipated this may happen and the boys made it to the airport on time.

We were able to leave the school with our project completely finished, well, almost.  The new brick classroom and two retaining walls were erected, a concrete floor was poured, the inside walls of the classroom were finished so they could later be painted, the concrete roof was dry, and a new brick bathroom with two stalls was constructed.  (This involved a very exciting shopping trip for toilets with Molly and our albail Seor Moreno).  The iron door and windows were paid for and were being fashioned by local workers in the community and the “techo de calimina” (tin roof) was going to be added by the fathers in the community.  However, when I showed up at the school on my very last day I arrived at an empty classroom that had no door or roof.  (I had been told the families would finish this at 7:00am.  I didn’t show up until 11:00am).  After questioning a few mothers I found out that Seor Fernandez got behind in his work and couldn’t have the door and windows done for a few days and the roof couldn’t be done until fathers were available (it was a job that required men).  The women were available to work, but they couldn’t put the roof on themselves and had to wait until that night when their husbands got home from work.  I stayed in Catorce de Febrero as late as I possibly could saying good-bye to families, delivering pictures we had printed for the families, handing out the T-shirts Jeanne brought, and helping finish up final tasks.  On this afternoon I was also invited to the birthday party of a one year old.  As an example of the force-feeding I experienced in Peru I was the ONLY person of all twenty people at the birthday party to be handed two pieces of cake at the same time.  I kept trying to give one back, but everyone in the room insisted I eat them.

The last thing we were able to accomplish was the first visit with a group of dentists that will be now serving the Catorce de Febrero community.  Molly picked up the dentists in downtown Lima and drove about an hour and a half by taxi with them to the school.  There the dentists did assessments for about thirty-five kids and handed out lots of free toothbrushes and toothpaste.  Most of the kids were VERY excited to see the dentists and were fighting to be first in line.  Only a few cried or refused to open their mouths.  It was fun to see the kids running around with their new toothbrushes saying how they were going to brush their teeth right away.  A few thought it was necessary to brush both before and after they ate.  I was just glad they were excited.  The dentists sent a budget to cover further procedures that some of the kids need, and we will be working with an NGO to provide funding for consistent dental care for the community.

It was disappointing not to be able to put all the new chairs, tables and bookshelves into the classroom and see the final final project, but we trust that the community will take care of this themselves.  They are excited about the school and are willing to put in time and energy to make it work.  We know they are not going to leave this brand new building full of brand new furniture without a door, roof, and window.  They really see the value in the school.  Seor Fernandez, the man who is making the door said, “I am excited to be helping.  I anticipate that one day my daughter, who is two, will be attending this school.”

We enjoyed our summer in Peru very much and are taking so much away from our experience.  We have mastered the tasks of mixing concrete with a shovel, bartering for the best price, and pouring hot tea water from a pot to a mug.  (We didn’t have a teakettle, so we improvised.)  We will forever remember the support, hospitality, and smiles of every man, woman, and child we met.  Peru, and specifically, San Juan de Lurigancho hold a place in our hearts.  It is really our goal now to make sustainable English teachers a reality.  We’ll keep you posted on this next goal.  Trust us, we are going to make it happen!

One other thing… we went to an Avril Lavigne concert in Lima.  She was pretty horrible.

On another note, Johis the kitty died.  It was a sad day for me.

In the process of writing this blog post I got an e-mail from Escarlet.  The school has a roof AND a door!

Old news about our times in Peru

Our time in Peru is really winding down and we are watching our construction project be completed before our eyes.  All that is left to be done is poor a concrete floor for the new classroom (we will have another faena day with the community this Sunday for that) and then complete a small bathroom.  Our workers are claiming that the bathroom will be done in three days, but they also claimed the whole project would be done by July 26.  Finishing details keep coming up and we are dealing with them as they come.   We have the pressure on our contractor to finish before we leave, and we will ensure it will all get done. We have arranged for the doors and windows to be manufactured and are furnishing the classroom with new tables and chairs.  Everyone will be very excited when the project is done.
This week we were invited to a celebration by the families of our two best English students.  It was fun to go out with the families and see them in a lively setting outside of the classroom.  We sampled great food from various regions of Peru and danced most of the night.  We all agreed it was one of our favorite experiences, since we could spend some quality time with our favorite families from Catorce de Febrero.
For the past week our neighborhood has been praying a novena for the Virgen del Carmen.  About 20 people gather every night to pray together and then have a little bit to eat.  Part of the service always involves singing and we, the gringos, were especially invited since both Michael and Gabe play the guitar.  We were called upon a few times to come up with an impromptu praise song and were able to sing “Lord prepare me to be a sanctuary” and “Light the Fire.”  The songs were received graciously even though most of the people didn’t know what we were saying since we sang in English.  It was a good experience for all of us.
July 28 is Independence Day in Peru and since it fell on a Thursday most people enjoyed a four day weekend.  The kids at our school participated in a parade in which they dressed as soldiers, nurses, and firefighters and marched with the Peruvian flag.  Our neighborhood had a big party on our street which was tons of fun.  There was a live band and tons of fireworks.  We stayed out dancing all night and went to bed at 7:00am.  The highlight of the fiesta for us was certainly the “vacas locas” that would show up every show often.  A vaca loca, or crazy cow, is essentially a cow costume covered in fireworks.  A man puts it on and then ignites the costume.  The cow runs throughout the street shooting fireworks while all the dancers run, dance, cheer, or, some brave souls, (Gabe Rysdahl)  follow closely behind  the cow hoping not to get caught with a spark.  We really need to bring them to the United States.
One particular highlight last week, for me (Molly) at least, was that our neighbors’ cat finally had her kittens!  As soon as I met this cat, Blanca, and found out she was pregnant I said I wanted to be there for the birth.  However, Blanca popped out the kittens at 4:00am and no one bothered to call me.  Blanca gave birth to 4 beautiful kittens that have been affectionately named Gabriel, Michael, Johis (Johis is the 14 year old daughter in the family that owns Blanca), and Mollycita.  About 18 hours after Blanca gave birth we had a scare when we found out she had another kitty in her that was dead.  Johis came running to get me and I saw a few family members gathered around Blanca, who was wincing with pain.  All we could see was a little kitty leg sticking out of her.  No one really knew what to do, it was just clear Blanca was in pain and needed some help.  The father of the family finally got down and tried pulling on the kitty to help Blanca out a bit.  There were four of us standing around watching and when he called for some help Johis and I were the ones who jumped up to help pin Blanca down.  We all thought Blanca would die if that kitty didn’t come out, which would mean little Mollycita would die, too!  The whole time I kept thinking cousin Matt, where are you?!  (For those of you who don’t know my cousin Matt, he is a vet.) We were unsuccessful in assisting Blanca, but when I returned the next morning Johis said Blanca had managed to wiggle the dead kitty out in the night and was doing fine.  Good, because another sister, Sherly, and I were ready to be feeding Mollycita with a syringe.
